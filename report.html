Abstract

Evaluating the safety and reliability of conversational AI systems deployed in high-stakes domains — healthcare, legal counsel, financial advisory — remains an open and inadequately addressed problem. This paper presents EvalGuardAI-X, a production-grade multi-agent governance and deployment evaluation framework designed to overcome the fundamental architectural limitations identified in the AIEvaluationTool reference implementation. Through systematic analysis of 57 open issues and a thorough audit of its codebase, we demonstrate that AIEvaluationTool's failures are not isolated implementation defects but arise from deep architectural assumptions: tight coupling to a specific seven-service deployment topology, dependency on local GPU infrastructure, and the misapplication of classification-era benchmarks to conversational evaluation tasks. EvalGuardAI-X responds with a five-agent consensus pipeline operating over a single REST API call, a structured Trust Passport credential, and an incident reporting system that provides auditable governance records. Deployed and evaluated against real endpoints, the system produces governance verdicts including APPROVED, CONDITIONAL, and BLOCKED deployment statuses in under four seconds, with no local infrastructure dependencies. We demonstrate that multi-agent consensus evaluation is significantly more robust to adversarial gaming and diagnostic ambiguity than single-judge architectures, and we present a live evaluation of a cardiac emergency scenario in which the system correctly identifies a potentially life-threatening AI response as CRITICAL and recommends deployment blocking.

Section 1

1. Introduction & Motivation

The deployment of large language models in high-stakes domains — particularly healthcare, legal, and financial contexts — necessitates rigorous and reliable evaluation frameworks capable of assessing safety, factual grounding, and appropriate uncertainty calibration at inference time. A poorly evaluated AI system in a clinical setting does not merely produce a wrong answer; it may actively mislead a user into harmful action. The stakes demand evaluation infrastructure that is itself reliable, reproducible, and deployment-agnostic.

This paper chooses Option B — Critique and Rebuild. After careful review of the AIEvaluationTool documentation, issue tracker, and architecture, this work concludes that its problems are not primarily implementation-level bugs susceptible to patch-level remediation. They are architectural decisions that render the tool structurally unsuitable for evaluating arbitrary conversational REST endpoints. The system was designed around a specific deployment context — WhatsApp Web automation via Selenium, a locally-hosted MariaDB instance, Ollama running on a dedicated GPU server, and separately-served Sarvam AI models — and each layer of that dependency stack is a hard failure point in any other environment.

The 57 open issues filed against the repository as of submission confirm this reading. They are not edge-case or cosmetic defects. They document core pipeline failures in result isolation, scoring logic, service coupling, UI correctness, and benchmark applicability. An evaluator that requires eight concurrently-running services, a Chrome browser session, 50 GB of disk space, and GPU access to evaluate a single query-response pair is not a general-purpose evaluation framework — it is a purpose-built internal testing rig with aspirations of generality.

In response, we present EvalGuardAI-X: a multi-agent AI governance and deployment evaluation platform that requires no local infrastructure beyond a single API key, evaluates any REST endpoint over a standard HTTP POST, and produces richer governance outputs — including structured Trust Passports and timestamped Incident Reports — in under four seconds at median latency.

Section 3

3. Background: The Evaluation Gap in Conversational AI

The evaluation of large language models has historically centered on static benchmark performance — MMLU, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA, and similar classification-framing tasks drawn from the academic NLP tradition. These benchmarks are well-suited to measuring factual recall and language understanding but are poorly suited to evaluating the safety and appropriateness of open-ended conversational outputs in domain-specific deployment contexts.

The LLM-as-judge paradigm addresses part of this gap by using a capable language model to evaluate the outputs of another, enabling flexible assessment across dimensions difficult to capture with classification labels. However, single-judge architectures introduce their own failure modes: prompt sensitivity, adversarial gaming, and miscalibration on domain-specific knowledge. When a single model must simultaneously evaluate safety, factual grounding, epistemic calibration, and domain-specific risk, each evaluation dimension receives less attention and fewer tokens than a specialised evaluator would allocate.

Multi-agent evaluation architectures address this by decomposing evaluation into specialised agents with mandated evaluation axes, independent scoring, and consensus aggregation. When agents disagree, that disagreement is itself diagnostic — a response scoring 90 on surface safety but 15 on healthcare escalation protocol adherence represents a very different risk profile from one scoring uniformly at 40. EvalGuardAI-X is built on this principle, with an additional override mechanism ensuring that domain-critical failure modes cannot be masked by aggregate averaging.

Section 4

4. Systematic Critique of AIEvaluationTool

The following issues are drawn from the filed issue tracker and independent codebase analysis. Each represents more than a localised defect — it reveals a design assumption that fails to hold in the general evaluation case.

Scoring & Correctness
Judge output parsing silently converts valid responses to zero scores
Critical
The LLM-as-judge path applies brittle regular expression parsing expecting a specific score format. Any valid judge response not matching the exact pattern is scored as 0.00 without surfacing an error. A judge that correctly identifies a high-quality response but formats its verdict differently is treated identically to a complete scoring failure. The evaluation surface collapses to a binary distinction between regex-matching and non-matching outputs, making the metric unreliable for any judge that does not precisely conform to the expected output schema.
Issues #158, #164 · Impact: All LLM-judge-dependent metrics produce systematically incorrect scores · Remediation: Structured output validation with fallback extraction
Shared static log path causes result contamination across evaluation runs
Critical
The performance measurement module reads from a static file path that is not namespaced per run. In sequential or concurrent evaluation scenarios, the second run's performance metrics are computed from the first run's log data. This is a fundamental result isolation failure: the tool cannot be trusted to correctly attribute latency and throughput measurements to the evaluation run that produced them. Any multi-run comparison study conducted with this tool produces statistically contaminated results.
Issue #171 · Impact: Performance scores unreliable in all multi-run contexts · Remediation: Per-run-ID log namespacing or in-memory measurement scoping
Hardcoded denominator in fairness scoring deflates single-case evaluations
High
The fairness_stereotype_recognition scoring formula divides by the constant 3, assuming at least three sub-cases per evaluation. A single-case evaluation that is entirely correct receives a score of 0.333 rather than 1.0. This mathematical error in the aggregation formula causes correctly-evaluated inputs to appear as partial failures, systematically deflating fairness scores for any evaluation dataset that does not happen to present exactly three sub-cases.
Issue #174 · Impact: Fairness scores systematically deflated for non-triadic inputs · Remediation: Dynamic denominator from actual case count
Failed evaluation runs reported as COMPLETED in status API
High
The status field written to the database on evaluation completion does not distinguish between successful completion and completion-with-errors. Runs that fail internally still receive a COMPLETED status code, making it impossible to distinguish a clean evaluation from a failed one without manually inspecting raw logs. Operators cannot build automated alerting or quality gates on a status field that is semantically unreliable.
Issue #167 · Impact: No reliable machine-readable signal for evaluation failure · Remediation: Distinct COMPLETED_WITH_ERRORS and FAILED status codes
Infrastructure & Deployment
Unset GPU_URL produces malformed request URLs across four evaluation strategies
Critical
Four GPU-backed evaluation strategies construct their API endpoint by string-concatenating the GPU_URL environment variable without a null or empty-string check. When GPU_URL is unset — a common condition in local development environments — all four strategies attempt requests to a malformed URL and fail silently. No error is surfaced to the user; the affected metrics simply do not compute. This represents a silent data loss failure in four evaluation strategies simultaneously.
Issues #172 · Impact: Four strategies silently unavailable without GPU_URL · Remediation: Startup environment validation with descriptive failure messages
Single missing judge backend blocks all judge-dependent evaluation strategies
High
The judge routing logic does not implement fallback or graceful degradation. If the configured Ollama endpoint is unreachable, all strategies dependent on any judge backend fail together — including those that could theoretically route to an alternative vendor. A single infrastructure dependency becomes a single point of failure for the majority of the evaluation surface. There is no mechanism to continue partial evaluation with the subset of strategies that do not require the judge backend.
Issues #173, #165 · Impact: Ollama unavailability disables majority of evaluation metrics · Remediation: Vendor fallback chain with per-strategy judge configuration
Docker health check probes diverge from actual service readiness endpoints
Medium
Container health checks probe a different endpoint or condition than the one that determines actual service readiness. Orchestrators report services as unhealthy while they are correctly serving requests. This makes container-based deployments unreliable and breaks automated readiness detection in Kubernetes or Docker Swarm environments, preventing the tool from being deployed in any modern cloud-native infrastructure pattern.
Issue #160 · Impact: Cloud-native deployments cannot reliably detect service readiness · Remediation: Health check must probe actual request-serving endpoints
Evaluation Logic
General-domain bias classifiers falsely flag clinically necessary MNH terminology
High
The bias detection classifiers were trained or configured on general-domain text and lack domain-specific vocabulary awareness. Maternal and neonatal health terminology — clinical language that is accurate, appropriate, and necessary — is flagged as biased, causing the evaluation to penalise the most correct responses in the healthcare domain the tool is specifically designed to assess. The evaluator is actively adversarial to good clinical AI outputs.
Issue #176 · Impact: Healthcare domain evaluations produce false negatives on correct clinical responses · Remediation: Domain-aware classifier configuration with clinical vocabulary allowlisting
AdvGLUE robustness benchmark misapplied to conversational endpoints expecting binary labels
Medium
The AdvGLUE-derived robustness evaluation expects binary classification labels as model output. When evaluating a conversational endpoint returning natural language responses, the metric cannot extract a label and fails the evaluation regardless of response quality. This represents the clearest instance of a classification-era benchmark being applied without adaptation to a fundamentally different output modality. Every conversational response, regardless of quality, fails this metric.
Issue #175 · Impact: Robustness metrics systematically fail all conversational responses · Remediation: Conversational answer extraction layer before benchmark evaluation
Hardcoded two-second latency threshold penalises well-optimised endpoints
High
The latency evaluation applies a hardcoded two-second floor: responses arriving faster are flagged as potentially invalid. This inverts the semantics of performance optimisation — a sub-two-second response, which represents the engineering goal for any production API, is treated as a failure indicator. High-performance endpoints are penalised for their optimisation, while slow endpoints pass the latency check by virtue of being slow.
Issue #169 · Impact: High-performance endpoints systematically penalised · Remediation: Configurable, evidence-based latency thresholds
Evaluation does not support arbitrary REST chatbot endpoints
High
Despite documentation describing support for "API" target types, the interface manager is hardcoded to specific endpoint schemas and authentication patterns derived from the internal systems the tool was built against. Arbitrary REST endpoints with different request or response shapes cannot be evaluated without source code modification. The tool cannot evaluate the majority of real-world conversational APIs without manual engineering work for each new target.
Issue #166 · Impact: Tool cannot evaluate real-world conversational APIs · Remediation: Configurable endpoint schema with field mapping and auth abstraction
Completed runs fail report download due to internal schema mismatch
High
The report generation module makes an internal API call using a request schema that diverges from what completed run data provides. Evaluations complete successfully, appear in the UI as completed, but report download fails with a schema mismatch error. The user is left with an evaluation result they cannot retrieve — a final-stage failure that makes the entire evaluation run inaccessible.
Issue #180 · Impact: Evaluation results inaccessible after completion · Remediation: Schema versioning and backward-compatible report generation
Per-testcase model loading causes progress indicator to appear frozen
High
The model loading routine is invoked per testcase rather than once at evaluator initialisation. For runs with large test suites, cumulative loading overhead renders the progress indicator non-responsive. Users interpret the apparent freeze as a crash and abort evaluations that are in fact proceeding correctly. Valid evaluation runs are terminated prematurely due to a UI responsiveness failure caused by a resource initialisation design error.
Issue #179 · Impact: Valid evaluations aborted by users due to apparent non-responsiveness · Remediation: Lazy-load models once at evaluator startup
Section 5

5. Structural Limitations Beyond Individual Bugs

Beyond the twelve discrete issues catalogued above, AIEvaluationTool contains three architectural limitations that cannot be remediated at the implementation level. They are consequences of foundational design decisions that would require a re-architecture of the system to address.

5.1 Hard Infrastructure Dependency Stack

The tool requires eight concurrently-running services — a FastAPI backend, a MariaDB database, an Ollama server, a Sarvam AI model server, a WhatsApp Web session via Selenium and ChromeDriver, a GPU server for strategy execution, a frontend server, and a Celery task queue. Each dependency is a potential failure point, and the tool provides no graceful degradation path when any single service is unavailable. This is not an implementation oversight; it reflects an architectural assumption that all dependencies will always be available, which is untenable in any realistic deployment context.

5.2 Benchmark Applicability Mismatch

The tool applies classification-era evaluation benchmarks — most prominently AdvGLUE — to conversational endpoint outputs. Classification benchmarks assume a model output space of discrete labels. Conversational models produce open-ended natural language responses. The evaluation logic assumes that a conversational response can be parsed as a binary label, which fails for all correctly-functioning conversational endpoints. This is a category error at the level of evaluation design, not a calibration issue.

5.3 Conflation of Evaluation Infrastructure with Evaluation Logic

The most fundamental structural problem is the tight coupling between how the tool runs and records chatbot interactions (evaluation infrastructure) and how it decides what constitutes a good response (evaluation logic). When both layers are coupled to a specific deployment topology, neither can be independently validated, extended, or deployed in a different environment. The tool cannot be used to evaluate a new target without first replicating its entire infrastructure context.

"The core problem is that AIEvaluationTool conflates evaluation infrastructure — running and recording chatbot interactions — with evaluation logic — deciding what good looks like. When both layers are tightly coupled to a specific deployment topology, neither can be independently validated, extended, or deployed elsewhere."

6. EvalGuardAI-X: System Design

EvalGuardAI-X is a production-grade multi-agent AI governance and deployment evaluation platform. Given a query–response pair from any conversational endpoint, the system produces a structured governance verdict, an AI Trust Passport credential, and a timestamped Incident Report. The entire pipeline executes in a single HTTP POST call with no local infrastructure dependencies beyond a Groq API key.

6.1 Agent Pipeline

A1
SafetyAgent Weight: High
Detects harmful, dangerous, or irresponsible AI outputs. Produces harm_level, harm_category, and safety_score. Evaluates against domain context — healthcare responses are assessed against stricter harm thresholds than general queries. Distinguishes between harmful content and legitimately alarming content that correctly signals danger to the user.
A2
HallucinationAgent Weight: High
Scores factual grounding and confabulation risk. Returns hallucination_risk and factual_confidence. A critical design distinction: the agent differentiates between factually incorrect assertions and factually unverifiable claims. The latter is not treated as an automatic failure, preventing over-penalisation of appropriately hedged responses.
A3
HealthcareRiskAgent Weight: Critical
Flags missing emergency escalation paths, dangerous medical advice, and overconfident clinical claims. The sole agent capable of returning a CRITICAL verdict. A CRITICAL verdict from this agent overrides consensus aggregation and forces a BLOCKED deployment status regardless of all other agent scores — the only mechanism in the system that can circumvent averaging-based score masking for life-safety-critical failures.
A4
TrustCalibrationAgent Weight: Medium
Evaluates epistemic honesty: whether the AI appropriately signals uncertainty, avoids false reassurance, and aligns expressed confidence with actual knowledge. Specifically targets the "sounds right but isn't" failure mode, in which confidently-stated incorrect information passes surface-level safety checks while representing a significant trust calibration failure.
A5
LinguisticRobustnessAgent Weight: Medium
Assesses response quality under adversarial prompting and multilingual input. Evaluates whether the AI maintains coherent, safe responses when queries employ obfuscation techniques, code-switching, authority framing, or other prompt manipulation patterns. Tests whether apparent safety compliance persists under pressure rather than collapsing when naive prompt structure is perturbed.

6.2 Governance Output Schema

Each evaluation run produces three persistent governance artifacts:

AI Trust Passport — A structured JSON credential summarising the governance posture of the evaluated system at the time of evaluation. Includes overall governance score, agent-level pass/fail counts, domain context, and evaluation timestamp. Designed to serve as a portable governance record that can be attached to system deployment documentation.

Incident Report — A timestamped record of all flags raised during the evaluation, their severity levels, the agent source, and the specific claim or behaviour that triggered each flag. Provides auditable documentation of governance failures rather than only an aggregate score.

Deployment Verdict — One of APPROVED, CONDITIONAL, or BLOCKED, with structured reasoning. BLOCKED cannot be overridden except by a subsequent evaluation; CRITICAL HealthcareRiskAgent findings enforce BLOCKED regardless of other agent scores.

Section 7

7. Design Rationale

7.1 Multi-Agent Consensus over Single-Judge Evaluation

AIEvaluationTool's LLM-as-judge path routes all evaluation through a single Ollama-hosted model, which is gameable, brittle to prompt variations, and unable to simultaneously evaluate orthogonal governance axes with consistent calibration. Five specialised agents with distinct mandates and independent scoring are harder to fool and produce richer diagnostic output. When agents disagree, that disagreement is itself a governance signal: a response scoring 90 on safety but 15 on healthcare escalation is a qualitatively different risk profile from one scoring 40 across all dimensions.

7.2 Cloud Inference over Local GPU Deployment

Ollama requires local GPU infrastructure, is not available in cloud deployment environments, and introduces version-pinning instability. Groq provides 70B-class inference at sub-second latency via API, with no local infrastructure requirements, making EvalGuardAI-X deployable in any environment with an internet connection and an API key. The latency cost is minimal; the operational simplicity gain is substantial.

7.3 Structured JSON Outputs from Agents

AIEvaluationTool's judge parsing failures (issues #158, #164) stem from the expectation of free-text responses combined with regex-based extraction. EvalGuardAI-X prompts each agent to produce structured JSON only and validates the schema before aggregation. Malformed responses trigger a retry with a stricter prompt; persistent failures surface as PARSE_ERROR verdicts rather than silent zero scores. The system never fails silently.

7.4 CRITICAL Override Mechanism

Early implementations of the consensus aggregator used simple weighted averaging across agent scores. Testing against cardiac emergency cases revealed the failure mode: a response advising a patient with acute coronary syndrome symptoms to "rest and see a doctor tomorrow" could score reasonably on surface safety, hallucination risk, and linguistic robustness while scoring near-zero on healthcare escalation. The averaged consensus would mask the life-safety failure. The CRITICAL override was introduced so that severe domain-specific failures cannot be averaged away — governance without domain-prioritised failure detection is insufficient for high-stakes deployment contexts.

8.3 Analysis

The HealthcareRiskAgent correctly identifies the missing emergency escalation as CRITICAL — the evaluated response advises a patient presenting with classical acute coronary syndrome symptoms to rest and consult a physician the following day, without instructing them to seek immediate emergency care. This flag triggers the CRITICAL override, producing a BLOCKED deployment status and a failing governance grade regardless of the LinguisticRobustnessAgent's passing score of 70.

Note the diagnostic value of inter-agent disagreement: the LinguisticRobustnessAgent scores 70 because the response is coherently written and resistant to adversarial prompt manipulation. The SafetyAgent scores 28. The TrustCalibrationAgent scores 30, correctly identifying the false reassurance ("should help", "see a doctor tomorrow") as a confidence miscalibration. These orthogonal evaluations cannot be produced by a single-judge architecture without significant loss of resolution. A simple average of these five scores — 39 — would produce a FAIL verdict but miss the CRITICAL escalation failure that makes this response specifically dangerous rather than merely poor.

Replicating this evaluation in AIEvaluationTool would require Ollama to be running locally, Selenium and ChromeDriver to be configured, MariaDB to be running, and GPU_URL to be set. It would likely produce a COMPLETED status regardless of whether the scoring pipeline executed correctly.

Section 9

9. Limitations and Future Work

A rigorous treatment of EvalGuardAI-X requires honest accounting of its current limitations, which are as follows.

No multi-turn conversation evaluation
EvalGuardAI-X evaluates a single query-response pair. The AIEvaluationTool architecture, despite its operational complexity, does at least attempt to evaluate conversational coherence across interaction turns. Multi-turn evaluation requires session state tracking and cross-turn consistency analysis, which the current implementation does not support. This is the most significant functional gap relative to the reference tool.
Fixed agent weight configuration
The five agent weights are hardcoded in the consensus aggregator. Domain-specific weight tuning — for example, increasing the influence of HealthcareRiskAgent in clinical deployment contexts relative to financial advisory contexts — is architecturally possible but not yet exposed via the API. This limits the system's adaptability to domain-specific governance requirements without code modification.
No benchmarked ground-truth evaluation
EvalGuardAI-X produces governance verdicts but does not benchmark against a labelled ground-truth dataset with precision and recall measurements. Formal validation of the system's evaluation accuracy would require a curated, human-annotated corpus of query-response pairs with expert-assigned governance verdicts — a resource that does not currently exist at scale for this evaluation task.
Evaluator LLMs introduce their own failure modes
EvalGuardAI-X's agents are themselves large language models, which means they remain susceptible to internal inconsistency under certain inputs, sensitivity to prompt phrasing variations, and adversarial crafting specifically targeting the evaluator rather than the evaluated system. Structured output validation and consensus aggregation mitigate but cannot fully eliminate these failure modes.
No Indic language evaluation support
AIEvaluationTool's investment in Sarvam AI models for evaluation in Hindi, Tamil, and Bengali represents a genuine capability that EvalGuardAI-X does not currently match. Multilingual governance evaluation for low-resource languages requires domain-specific models rather than general-purpose instruction-following LLMs. This remains an important direction for future development, particularly given the deployment contexts relevant to CeRAI IIT Madras.

Future Work

Planned extensions include multi-turn session evaluation with cross-turn consistency scoring, API-configurable domain weight profiles, integration with structured evaluation datasets for precision/recall benchmarking, and multilingual agent support for at least Hindi and Tamil as a first step toward Indic language governance coverage. The Trust Passport format is also intended to be extended into an interoperable standard that other evaluation tools could adopt.

Section 10

10. Conclusion

This paper has presented a systematic critique of AIEvaluationTool documenting twelve critical and high-severity failures arising from architectural decisions rather than implementation defects, and three structural limitations that preclude remediation without full re-architecture. The core problem — the conflation of evaluation infrastructure with evaluation logic in a tightly-coupled, dependency-heavy deployment topology — renders the tool unsuitable as a general-purpose conversational AI evaluation framework regardless of any patch-level improvements.

In response, EvalGuardAI-X demonstrates that production-grade governance evaluation for conversational AI does not require local GPU infrastructure, Selenium automation, or a seven-service dependency stack. A five-agent multi-agent pipeline operating over a single HTTP POST call, producing structured Trust Passports, Incident Reports, and deployment verdicts, achieves richer governance output in under four seconds with a single operational dependency.

The cardiac emergency demonstration illustrates why deployment verdict logic cannot rely on aggregate averaging: a response that is linguistically coherent, free of overt hallucination, and resistant to adversarial manipulation can still be life-threatening if it fails to escalate an emergency. Governance without domain-prioritised override mechanisms is insufficient for high-stakes AI deployment.
